When Sleep Becomes a Struggle

Poor sleep affects everything. Concentration slips. Emotions feel harder to manage. Even small challenges feel bigger than they should. And yet, the more you focus on trying to sleep, the more elusive it becomes — which is one of the crueller aspects of insomnia. The effort itself becomes part of the problem.

I know this from personal experience. Hypnotherapy was part of my own recovery from insomnia, and that experience shapes how I work with clients. It's not abstract for me. I understand what it feels like to dread bedtime, to lie awake watching hours pass, to get up the next morning knowing the day ahead will be harder than it should be.

The good news is that sleep is not a habit that's permanently broken — it's a natural state your body knows how to reach. Hypnotherapy works by addressing the anxiety and mental patterns that have disrupted your relationship with sleep, and gently restoring the conditions your mind and body need to rest.

You may recognise these experiences

  • Difficulty falling asleep, even when you're exhausted
  • Waking repeatedly through the night
  • A mind that won't switch off — replaying the day, worrying about tomorrow
  • Clock-watching and calculating how many hours you have left
  • Dreading bedtime because you expect to lie awake
  • Feeling unrefreshed no matter how long you sleep
  • Relying on sleep aids, alcohol or screens to wind down
  • Exhaustion affecting your mood, work, relationships and health

Why the Mind Resists Sleep

Sleep problems rarely have a single cause — but they almost always involve the mind's threat-detection system, the same system responsible for anxiety. When that system is activated, even subtly, the brain shifts into a state of alertness that is the opposite of what sleep requires.

Over time, the bedroom itself, the ritual of getting into bed, even the thought of sleep, can become associated with wakefulness and frustration. The brain, trying to be helpful, starts treating sleep as a problem to be solved rather than a natural process to be allowed. That shift in relationship to sleep is at the heart of most chronic insomnia.

Hypnotherapy addresses this by working directly with the subconscious patterns that maintain the sleep-anxiety cycle — not by forcing or sedating, but by restoring a sense of safety and ease around sleep.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many sessions will I need?

Most clients notice meaningful improvement within 3–5 sessions. Some notice a difference after the very first. We review progress regularly and adapt the approach to what's working for you.

Does hypnotherapy actually work for insomnia?

There is a growing body of research supporting hypnotherapy's effectiveness for sleep difficulties. A 2018 review published in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine found hypnotherapy a promising treatment for insomnia. Practically speaking, the deep relaxation of hypnotherapy directly counters the arousal state that prevents sleep.

Is this the same as being put to sleep?

No. Hypnosis is not sleep — it's a focused, relaxed, wakeful state. You remain fully aware throughout the session and in complete control. Many clients describe it as the most rested they've felt in months, which is itself a good sign for what's possible at home.

What if I've had sleep problems for years?

Long-standing sleep difficulties can absolutely respond to hypnotherapy, though it may take a few more sessions to shift deeply ingrained patterns. The fact that something has been present for a long time doesn't mean it's permanent — it just means it's well established, and we work to gently establish something better in its place.

Can sessions be online?

Yes. Online sessions via Zoom are available and often work particularly well for sleep clients — you can settle in the comfort of your own home, and there's no journey at the end of the session to interrupt the calm. Sessions are also available in person in Reigate, Surrey.

Do I need to stop any medication?

No. Hypnotherapy works safely alongside medication and other treatments. Always continue with anything prescribed by your GP. If you're hoping to reduce reliance on sleep medication over time, we can discuss that as part of your goals — but any changes to medication should always be made with your doctor's guidance.
